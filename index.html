Reyna Grande’s Growth as a Writer

This section explains how Reyna Grande grows from an immigrant student who struggles with language into a future writer who can use memory, rejection, and reading to build her voice.

Reyna Grande’s growth as a writer begins with identity, silence, and persistence. Early in The Distance Between Us, Reyna uses writing to remember where she comes from: “I wrote that even though I was now living far away from Mami and my country, I hadn't forgotten where I came from” (203). This sentence shows that writing is not just a school assignment for Reyna; it is a way to protect her memory, her mother, and her Mexican identity.

However, Reyna does not yet have enough English to defend her own voice. When her teacher rejects her book, Reyna explains, “I wanted to tell her she'd made a mistake and she must look at it again, but I didn't have the English words to say what I thought how I felt-so I said nothing at all” (204). This moment shows that Reyna has thoughts and feelings, but language keeps her silent.

Instead of letting rejection stop her, Reyna turns it into a goal: “One day, I promised myself, thinking about Mr. López's words, I will write a book that won't be rejected, one that will make my father proud” (206). Later, reading helps her build the English power she needs: “Thanks to my addiction to reading, I had learned English well enough to be enrolled in regular English classes starting in the new school year” (234). Reyna’s growth shows that she did not become a writer suddenly. She moved from silence to language, from rejection to motivation, and from immigrant memory to a writer’s voice.

Growth Pattern

1

Identity: Reyna writes to remember Mexico, Mami, and where she comes from.

2

Silence: English limits her ability to defend her own work.

3

Persistence: Rejection becomes a reason to keep writing.

4

Language Power: Reading helps her move into regular English classes.
