Introduction Type: Background Information

Introduction: A Semester of Reading, Writing, and Reflection

Before taking AmLa 80, I thought an English class was mainly about grammar, vocabulary, and writing essays correctly. During this semester, I learned that academic writing is also about thinking carefully, using evidence, and connecting a text to real life. Through The Distance Between Us, reading journals, low-stakes writing assignments, TED Talk activities, Google Slides, and the Cajita Project, I started to understand immigration in a deeper way. This class helped me grow not only as an English student, but also as a person who can think more seriously about sacrifice, family, identity, and opportunity.

Prompt Section 1

Reyna Grande: From Undocumented Immigrant to Award-Winning Author

Reyna Grande's life story shows growth mindset because she did not let her painful childhood become the end of her future. In The Distance Between Us, Reyna begins as a young girl in Mexico who is separated from her parents. Her parents leave for the United States, and Reyna has to grow up with confusion, loneliness, and a feeling of being left behind. This part of her life matters because it shows that migration does not only affect the person who leaves. It also affects the children who wait, miss their parents, and try to understand why the family has been broken apart.

Prompt Section 2

My Growth: From AmLa 80 Student to AmLa 90 Student

At the beginning of AmLa 80, I was not fully confident in academic writing. I could understand some ideas, but it was difficult to explain them deeply in English. Many times, my first reaction was to summarize the story instead of analyzing it. During the semester, I learned that a strong response needs more than retelling events. A strong response needs a clear idea, specific evidence, and explanation.

Reading Journals

Reading journals helped me practice thinking while reading. I learned to choose important moments from the book and explain why those moments matter. This helped me move from simple summary to analysis. For example, when I wrote about family separation or Reyna's struggle with identity, I had to think about the meaning behind the event, not only what happened in the chapter.

Quote Sandwich

Quote sandwich was one of the most useful writing strategies for me. Before this class, I sometimes used quotations without enough explanation. In AmLa 80, I learned that a quotation needs context before it and analysis after it. The quote should not do all the work by itself. I need to explain how the evidence supports my point.

Low-Stakes Writing Assignments

Low-stakes writing assignments helped me practice without feeling that every mistake would destroy my grade. These smaller assignments made writing less stressful. They also helped me prepare for bigger essays because I could test my ideas before writing a final version.

Google Slides and Class Activities

Google Slides and class activities helped me organize the ideas from the class. They gave me vocabulary, examples, and structure. When I looked at class slides, I could see how immigration connects to push and pull factors, sacrifice, the American Dream, and identity. These materials helped me use class language more clearly in my writing.

TED Talk Activities

TED Talk activities helped me see immigration from more than one perspective. The book gave me Reyna's personal story, while the TED Talks and class sources helped me think about larger issues, such as opportunity, language, education, and how immigrants are judged by society. These activities helped me connect personal stories to bigger social ideas.

Now I feel more prepared for AmLa 90 because I understand what I need to do as a student. I still need to improve my grammar and sentence variety, but I know how to start with a main idea, use evidence, and explain my thinking. AmLa 80 helped me build a stronger academic foundation.

Prompt Section 3

Text to Self: How My Perspective on Immigrants Changed

My perspective on immigrants changed during this semester. Before this class, I already knew that immigration was difficult, but I often thought about it in a practical way: jobs, money, school, legal status, and language. After reading The Distance Between Us and completing class activities, I started to see immigration as something more emotional and complicated. Immigration can create opportunity, but it can also create separation, guilt, pressure, and identity conflict.

Reyna's story helped me understand that immigrants and immigrant families often carry invisible struggles. A child may look normal at school, but that child may be carrying fear, family pressure, language problems, or memories from another country. This changed how I think about immigrant success. Success does not only mean getting a better job or living in a better place. Success can also mean surviving a difficult transition, keeping hope, and still trying to build a future.

My Cajita Project also helped me connect the class materials to myself. The objects in my box were not random decorations. They represented different parts of the immigrant experience. A dollar bill represented opportunity and the pressure to improve life. A pen represented education and the hope for a more stable future. A tea bag represented the culture and memories that immigrants may carry with them. A car key represented movement, independence, and future goals. These objects helped me understand that migration is not only about leaving one country. It is also about carrying memories, losing comfort, and trying to build a new identity.

My Main Realization

I learned that immigrants are not only people who move. They are people who sacrifice, adapt, remember, and rebuild. This is why the immigrant experience should be understood with more patience and respect.

Prompt Section 4

Advice for the AmLa 80 Teacher and Future Students

Three Pieces of Advice for the AmLa 80 Teacher

1. Give more model examples before major assignments.

Model examples are valuable because students can see what a strong answer looks like. Sometimes students understand the topic, but they do not know how much detail or analysis is expected. A sample paragraph or sample project section can reduce confusion.

2. Review final project requirements earlier.

The final project has many parts, so reviewing the requirements earlier would help students plan their work. If students know the project structure sooner, they can save class materials, pictures, and notes during the semester instead of trying to remember everything at the end.

3. Keep using low-stakes writing.

Low-stakes writing is useful because it gives students practice before major essays. It also helps students become less afraid of writing in English. These small assignments helped me build confidence and test my ideas.

Three Pieces of Advice for Future AmLa 80 Students

1. Do not only summarize the story.

Summary is necessary, but analysis is more important. Future students should explain why a moment matters and how it connects to immigration, identity, sacrifice, or family. This will make their reading journals and essays stronger.

2. Save class materials and notes.

Google Slides, reading journals, TED Talk notes, and low-stakes writings can help with bigger assignments later. If students save these materials, the final project will be easier because they already have evidence of their learning.

3. Ask questions before the due date.

If an assignment is confusing, students should ask early. Waiting until the last day creates stress and usually makes the writing weaker. Asking questions is not a weakness; it is part of becoming a better student.

I still have many things to improve, but I now know how to use evidence, explain my ideas, and connect class materials to my own thinking. As I move forward, I want to keep improving my writing and become a student who can express deeper ideas with more confidence.
